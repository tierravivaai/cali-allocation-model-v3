# Option A: Inverted United Nations Scale of Assessment Model

## Introduction

- What COP 16/2 says in relation to the allocation and disbursement of funding

## The Concept

That inversion of the UN Scale of Assessment could form the basis for an allocation formula meeting the principles and purposes of allocation and disbursement established by the COP in paras 1 and 18 of the Annex to Decision 16/2 to the effect that 
the allocation formula should allocated in a "fair, equitable, transparent, accountable and gender-responsive manner" and in particular that: 

"Funding should support the realization of the objectives of the Convention in developing
country Parties, in particular the least developed countries and small island developing States, and
plans; contribute to scientific research on biodiversity; benefit indigenous peoples and local
Parties with economies in transition, especially the conservation and sustainable use of biodiversity,
including through the delivery of activities described in national biodiversity strategies and action; ; contribute to scientific research on biodiversity; benefit indigenous peoples and local"

## The Model

The basic concept is to meet the criteria established by COP while adding in a requirement for simplicity in model interpretation

### Version 1: Raw Inversion of the UN Scale of Assessment

Version 1 of the Inverted UN Scale model was presented to members of the AHTEG on the 26th of February 2026 and the online repository containing the basis for the model is available at [Insert]().

- show the UN scale of assessment
- show the raw inversion of the UN scale of assessment (1 divided by the share) to show how the model works in a transparent way
- show graphics of the distribution before inversion and after inversion. For human interpretation it may be best to show raw distribution on percentages and then on log scale to show the distribution more clearly. Note that
people may not understand or trust log scales and so this needs to be dealt with carefully.

- report the consequences for distribution of the raw inversion in terms that Parties can easily appreciate. As expected, the raw inversion successfully allocates funding to developing countries and least developed countries. However, in doing so 
in a way that creates extremes in allocation between developing country Parties that does not take account of the biodiversity stewardship responsibilities of biodiversity rich middle income countries and small island developing states. As such 
it fails to meet the criteria of being equitable in its allocation to all developing country Parties. 
- Nevetheless, the simplicity and transparency of the use of inverted UN scale suggested that a modified approach could smooth out some of the imbalances in the distribution while still meeting the criteria of being equitable to all developing country Parties.

- Raw inversion had the useful consequence of exposing that biodiversity rich middle income countries and small island developing states have very low shares on the UN scale of assessment. This is a consequence of the fact that the UN scale of assessment is based on economic capacity and not on biodiversity stewardship responsibility. 
This is an important insight that can be used to inform the design of a model that takes account of both economic capacity and biodiversity stewardship responsibility. The solution identified for this issue was to instroduce two adjustments for 
a) Terrestrial Stewardship (TSAC) and 
b) SIDS Ocean Stewardship (SOSAC). 

These adjustments are designed to recognise the biodiversity stewardship responsibilities of biodiversity rich middle income countries and small island developing states while still maintaining the simplicity and transparency of the inverted UN scale of assessment as the foundation of the model.

- Based on the experience and lessons learned from version 1, version 2 was prepared with two key adjustments. 

1. The UN Scale was inverted by grouping Parties into bands.
2. Stewardship was explicitly recognised by introucing TSAC and SOSAC as scaleable adjustments to the allocations created by the banded inversion of the UN Scale.

#### Stewardship Adjustments (TSAC and SOSAC)

The concept of biodiversity stewardship is central to the allocation model. Under the Global Biodiversity Framework Parties have adopted a set of x goals and x targets and have prepared or are preparing national biodiversity strategies and action plans (NBSAPs) to implement the GBF. The implementation of the GBF will require significant funding and it is important that the allocation of funding takes account of the biodiversity stewardship responsibilities of Parties.
and, in many cases national biodiversity finance plans. The baseline of sovereign equality recognises that all Parties to the Convention have biodiversity stewardship responsibilities. However those responsibilities are not equal and the allocation of funding should take account of the biodiversity stewardship responsibilities of Parties.

- For some parties geographic size and the extent of their biodiversity stewardship responsibilities are likely to be correlated. For example, for terrestrial biodiversity stewardship, the larger the land area of a Party the greater the biodiversity stewardship responsibilities of that Party. 
For ocean biodiversity stewardship, the larger the ocean area of a Party the greater the biodiversity stewardship responsibilities of that Party. However, for some Parties, such as small island developing states (SIDS), their biodiversity stewardship responsibilities may be disproportionately large relative to their geographic size. 
As such, it is important that the allocation model takes account of both geographic size and the distinctiveness of SIDS Parties in recognising biodiversity stewardship responsibilities.

Biodiversity stewardship under the Global Biodiversity Framework can be considered to involve the combination of the actions and resources required to implement NBSAPS within their geographic jurisdictions. 
The ecosystems, biodiversity and indigenous peoples and local communities conducting local stewardship within the geographic area encompassed by a Party and the challenges this may involve may vary considerably
between Parties in terms of dryland, mountain, freshwater, forest, marine and coastal ecosystems, species richness within those ecosystems.  
Above all, the ability of Parties to meet their biodiversity stewardship responsibilities will vary considerably between Parties.



Stewardship responsibilities, and the capacity of Parties to exercise those responsibilties, may vary considerably within that geographic space.






- TSAC and the widely used FAOSTAT land area dataset were used to create a scaleable adjustment for terrestrial stewardship. The logic and rationale for this adjustment should be clearly explained and the resulting distribution of shares across Parties should be shown.
- SOSAC and the list of eligible SIDS was used to create a scaleable adjustment for SIDS ocean stewardship. The logic and rationale for this adjustment should be clearly explained and the resulting distribution of shares across Parties should be shown. Notably a decision was
made to allocate any percentage set for SOSAC equally among all eligible SIDS rather than using a more complex and contestable approach involving the calculation of marine areas and potential inequitable outcomes this could produce among SIDS Parties. This approach also met
the criteria of simplicity, transparency and equity while recognising the distinctiveness of SIDS Parties and their biodiversity stewardship responsibilities.

The creation of version 2 allowed for the development of a set of transparent scenarios for the allocation of funding at different scales between Parties.
